Project Background and Motivation


Introduction


We chose to create a predictor of the stock price of wheat because of the ever-pervasive fluctuation in food prices. Wheat is among one of the most fundamental agricultural commodities in the United States, so understanding and ultimately predicting the price of this commodity will allow us to understand an essential part of our economic ecosystem. Our team has diverse backgrounds in engineering and science, so we wanted to choose a topic that has profound global implications. One of the grand challenges identified by the leaders of the U.N. and world bank is the shortage of food in our ever growing population. We thought it would be interesting for us to be able to build predictive forecasting of the stock price of this key commodity.

Inflation EDA



A quick plotting of the percent change in inflation compared to wheat prices shows unpromising results, as a correlation isn't clearly visible (left). Tryign to plot a linear regression on this set of data gives us the same results. The linear regression is so uncertain, as evidenced by the large uncertainties, that it doesn't seem like a clear indicator of wheat prices.

Corn Price EDA


Description

When simply graphing corn price vs. wheat price, we can see that there is a positive correlation as wheat prices rise when corn prices do (left). Fitting a linear regression line on this data provides a line with relatively low uncertainty, which is the light blue shade around the regression (right). This indicates that corn price is a good feature to include in the wheat price model.

Sorghum EDA


On the graph to the left, the wheat price is graphed vs. the sorghum price in that same month. Looking at the graph, we see a positive correlation. We then fit a linear regression onto the same data, this time graphed as a scatter plot for clarity (right). The relatively small shaded areas indicates lower uncertainties, which indicates that sorghum price is a good indicator for wheat price.

Temperature EDA


When looking at temperature, the first step was to compare the average monthly temperature of all the states with wheat price. The results are hardly encouraging, as there seems to be little to no correlation. This makes sense because many states don't even produce wheat, and the winter months when wheat has not been planted should also have no effect on wheat prices. Therefore, the comparisons were refined, which was done by only examining the top 5 wheat-producing states in the US.




Here, the correlations between temperature and wheat price continue to be discouraging. No clear correlation can be seen, as the data all tends to be evenly distributed along the bottom of the graph. Averaging the data for the 5 states (right) doesn't appear to make a huge difference. As a last step toward refining the data to compare, the data was constricted to the months of March through October, which marks the beginning and end of the wheat growing season.



This further refined comparison provides no new results, suggesting that temperature does not have a large effect on wheat prices.

Precipitation EDA


When looking at precipitation, the first step was to compare the averaged cumulative rainfall of all the states with wheat price. The results are suggest little to no correlation. However, this data considers many states that don't even produce wheat, and the winter months when wheat isn't being grown. To account for these factors, the data was then cut to only examine the data of the top 5 wheat-producing states in the US.





Here, the correlations between precipitation and wheat price still aren't very correlated. The data points tend to be clumped in the the bottom left corner of the graph (left). There are some fairly large outliers in this dataset, but a clear correlation can't be found, regardless. Averaging the data for the 5 states (right) doesn't have much impact either. As a last step toward refining the data to compare, the data was constricted to the months of March through October, which marks the beginning and end of the wheat growing season.



This further refined comparison provides no new results, suggesting that precipitation does not have a large effect on wheat prices, much like temperature. However, looking at the very tops of all four of these graphs, there appears to be a slightly negative correlation, albeit a weak one. This very small correlation led to preciptation's inclusion in our later model.

Palmer Drought Severity Index EDA


The Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI) is a way to quantifiably describe drought conditions of a specific region. The index ranges from -4.0 to 4.0. In this index, -4.0 represents a very severe drought, 0 represents mild conditions, and a 4.0 represents abnormally wet conditions. The map above shows the PDSI by state in the year 2005, which was recorded to be a hard drought year for certain states such as Arkansas and Wyoming.





The above graphs show the PDSI of the top 5 states versus the wheat price, individually (left) and averaged together (right). There is not a strong correlation between these two factors, and nowhere near the correlation between wheat and corn/sorghum. However, in general, as the PDSI increases, the likelihood of having extremely high wheat prices decreases as well. This indicates that there is possibly a small negative correlation. Although it isn't close to being linear, this small trend warranted more investigation by using it in the predictive model.
